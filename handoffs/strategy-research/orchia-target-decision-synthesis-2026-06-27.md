# Orchia Target Decision Synthesis

Date: 2026-06-27  
Purpose: Integrate the six strategy research tracks into a decision memo for Orchia's near-term target.

## Executive Decision

The best target is a **hybrid**, but the product should not be marketed as a hybrid.

Customer-facing wedge:

> Orchia helps operator-led businesses run safe AI workflows that create revenue outcomes.

Best first customer-facing version:

> Orchia helps skilled product sellers improve one listing, find 20 likely buyers, and run approved follow-up without becoming full-time marketers.

Underlying product truth:

> Orchia is a visual control plane for delegated AI work: tasks, owners, approvals, logs, review gates, pause/resume, and repeatable workflow presets.

This is the strongest path because it keeps the technical asset you already built, but avoids pitching a broad "agent OS" or blank workflow builder before there is enough market pull. The wedge gives investors a buyer, a measurable outcome, and a fast validation path.

## What The Six Reports Agree On

The research reports independently converge on five points:

1. **Pure generic is too broad.** A generic AI workflow/task board can fit many use cases, but blank workflow builders are crowded and hard to sell without a very urgent first job.
2. **Pure ecommerce is too easy to commoditize.** If Orchia becomes AI listing/copy generation, Shopify, eBay, Etsy, Klaviyo, Canva, and generic AI tools can absorb it.
3. **The strongest product shape is a preset control plane.** Sell a concrete workflow outcome; make the control plane the reason it is trusted, reviewable, and repeatable.
4. **Revenue-adjacent workflows monetize faster.** Active sellers, agencies, B2B operators, and people close to pipeline/sales pay faster than users buying generic productivity.
5. **The first 2-4 weeks should be evidence-driven.** Run seller pilots and control-plane trials in parallel; let payments, repeat usage, and urgency choose the final public story.

## Why Not Lead With Generic

The generic idea has breadth, but breadth is not the same as urgency.

The generic workflow/control-plane can fit:

- AI coding teams.
- Solo founders managing agent teams.
- AI automation agencies.
- Research ops.
- Design/content production.
- QA/release gates.
- Data labeling and AI eval queues.
- Support procedure automation.
- Personal "operating company" simulation.

But the market already has strong generic workflow and agent platforms: n8n, Zapier, Make, LangGraph/LangSmith, CrewAI, GitHub Copilot coding agent, OpenAI Codex, Claude Code, Devin, Factory, DevPlan, and many YC-backed agent infrastructure companies.

Generic Orchia should be the engine and possibly the developer/community story, but not the first investor/customer promise.

Best generic framing if needed:

> Orchia is the visual control plane for delegated AI work.

Avoid:

> Generic task board for agents.

## Why Not Lead With Broad Ecommerce

The ecommerce/creator pain is real, but the broad category is dangerous.

Weak versions:

- AI marketing for Etsy sellers.
- AI listing generator.
- AI content machine for creators.
- AI-native Shopify.
- Autopilot your brand.

Those are crowded, platform-exposed, and likely to attract price-sensitive users.

The strong version is narrower:

> Capacity-aware sales workflows for skilled product makers.

This wedge is not about creating more content. It is about helping a maker move from a real product to a small, approved sales loop:

1. Diagnose why the product is not selling.
2. Improve the listing/trust/story.
3. Find likely buyer segments or partners.
4. Draft authentic outreach/follow-up.
5. Require approval before sending/publishing.
6. Track replies, interest, orders, waitlist signups, and capacity consumed.

The strongest MVP promise from the ecommerce report:

> Improve one listing and find 20 likely buyers.

## Best First Wedge

Recommended first wedge:

> Product photo + notes + capacity -> listing improvement -> 20 likely buyers -> 5 approved outreach drafts -> follow-up tracker.

This is good because:

- It starts with a concrete object/product, not vague marketing.
- It is small enough for a concierge MVP.
- It can produce before/after artifacts.
- It creates measurable intermediate outcomes even before revenue attribution is perfect.
- It makes the control plane visibly valuable: draft, review, approve, send, follow up, learn.

Best early customer:

- Active Etsy/Shopify/eBay/local/craft-fair sellers with real products and proof of effort.
- Small specialty product sellers with inventory or high-ticket/custom products.
- Sellers with some sales, store traffic, event interest, or abandoned follow-up.

Avoid at first:

- Pure hobbyists.
- Dropshippers.
- Creators with no product or no intent to sell.
- Users who only want cheap AI copy.

## Fastest Revenue Channel

The monetization report suggests the fastest payers may not be the individual sellers themselves, but **agencies and operators** serving them.

Fastest likely payers:

1. AI automation agencies and ecommerce/growth operators.
2. Active ecommerce sellers with existing sales or ad/tool spend.
3. B2B/outreach operators who already buy lead-generation tools.
4. AI-heavy developers and small dev teams.
5. Beginner creators with no revenue.

Practical implication:

Run two go-to-market tests at once:

- Seller-facing promise: "Improve one listing and find 20 likely buyers."
- Agency/operator-facing promise: "Deliver approved AI revenue workflows for clients with visible status, review gates, and reusable templates."

If agencies pay faster, use them as the distribution channel while keeping the skilled-seller workflow as the product demo.

## Pricing Hypotheses

Near-term tests:

- Free diagnostic: "Why is this product/listing not selling?"
- One-off sprint: `$99-$299` for one product workflow.
- Monthly seller workflow: `$79-$149/month`.
- Serious seller/local custom maker concierge: `$299-$500/month`.
- Agency/team plan: `$399-$799/month`.

Do not start with a blanket revenue share.

If testing success pricing, make it:

- Optional.
- Attributed.
- Capped.
- Tied to a specific workflow, campaign, lead, or listing.

Good usage units:

- Completed workflow.
- Listing improved/published.
- Buyer targets found.
- Outreach batch approved/sent.
- Qualified reply or lead.
- Review cycle completed.

Bad usage units:

- Raw tokens.
- Tiny internal agent steps.
- Percentage of total store GMV without incrementality proof.

## Investor Story

Best YC/AI House version:

> Orchia builds safe visual AI workflows for operator-led businesses. We are starting with skilled product sellers because the pain is urgent and measurable: they can make real products, but cannot reliably turn them into listings, buyer discovery, follow-up, and repeat sales. Under the hood, Orchia is an auditable agent control plane with task ownership, approval gates, review, logs, pause/resume, and reusable workflow presets.

Why this is fundable:

- Specific buyer.
- Urgent pain.
- Revenue-adjacent value.
- Measurable workflow outputs.
- Real technical substrate.
- Can start concierge-style.
- Can expand horizontally after proving one vertical.

Investor objections to prepare for:

- Is this two companies?
- Why will solo sellers pay enough?
- Why won't Shopify/Etsy/eBay/Klaviyo own this?
- How do you avoid becoming generic AI copywriting?
- How do you prove revenue attribution?
- What is the security/safety model?
- What is the wedge that expands into a big company?

Disciplined answer:

> Customers buy the seller workflow product. The control plane is why it is safe, inspectable, and extensible.

## Alternate High-ACV Wedges

The alternative-niches report surfaced strong options if the seller wedge fails or if the company wants higher ACV from day one.

Top alternate:

### GovCon Capture And Proposal Ops

Why attractive:

- High contract values.
- Deadline-heavy, document-heavy workflows.
- Strong need for evidence, review, compliance, assignments, and audit trails.
- Higher willingness to pay than solo sellers.

First workflow:

> Capture Command Center: opportunity search, bid/no-bid, compliance matrix, SME questions, past-performance retrieval, red-team review, and submission readiness.

Risk:

- Slower sales.
- Compliance expectations.
- Proposal AI market already active.

### AI Implementation Consultants / Agencies

Why attractive:

- Fastest route to revenue.
- They already sell AI workflows.
- They need client-facing control, status, logs, and reusable templates.

First workflow:

> Client Automation Control Room: intake, workflow map, risk register, build tasks, testing, approval gates, launch, and weekly client report.

Risk:

- Could become agency tooling rather than a venture-scale vertical unless workflow data and templates compound.

## 30-Day Evidence Plan

### Week 1

Interview:

- 10 skilled product sellers.
- 5 ecommerce/growth/AI automation agencies.
- 5 AI coding power users or small teams.

Create:

- One landing page for the seller workflow.
- One demo script showing the control-plane board.
- One intake form for product photo/listing/capacity.

Decision metric:

- Which group has urgent pain and agrees to a paid or high-commitment pilot?

### Week 2

Run concierge pilots:

- 3 seller workflows.
- 2 agency/operator workflows.
- 1 dev-agent coordination trial.

Collect:

- Before/after listings.
- Buyer target lists.
- Approved messages.
- Replies or interest signals.
- Hours saved.
- Quotes.
- Payment/deposit/LOI attempts.

Decision metric:

- At least 3 paid pilots or strong LOIs from one segment.

### Week 3

Build a clickable/working MVP around the strongest pull:

- Seller workflow if sellers pay.
- Agency workflow if agencies pay.
- Dev-agent control plane if teams use repeatedly.

Keep the same core UI:

- Intake.
- Task board.
- Drafts.
- Approvals.
- Execution/follow-up.
- Results.

Decision metric:

- Repeat usage, not just admiration.

### Week 4

Prepare funding package:

- 90-second demo video.
- Landing page.
- 3 case studies.
- Metrics table.
- User quotes.
- Competitive answer.
- Pricing hypothesis.
- YC/AI House application narrative.

## Kill / Pivot Criteria

Pause seller wedge if:

- Sellers love the idea but will not pay even for a small pilot.
- Demand is mostly from beginners with no product quality or buying intent.
- The value collapses into generic copy/listing generation.
- Users refuse to approve/send/follow up because trust is too low.

Pause generic control-plane wedge if:

- Users say it is cool but would only use it inside GitHub/Linear/Codex.
- Teams do not use it on multiple days.
- It does not prevent real coordination pain.
- Nobody wants to pay outside internal power users.

Lean into agency/GovCon if:

- Agencies pay quickly for client workspaces and workflow packs.
- GovCon/proposal users show willingness to pay for bid/proposal control.
- Higher-ACV users value audit trails and review more than consumer/SMB users.

## Final Recommendation

For investor success rate, lead with:

> Safe visual AI workflows for skilled product sellers.

For product architecture, build:

> A generic delegated-work control plane with presets.

For first MVP, test:

> Improve one listing and find 20 likely buyers.

For fastest revenue, also test:

> Agency client automation control room.

For backup high-ACV vertical, keep warm:

> GovCon capture/proposal operations.

This gives Orchia the best blend of fundability, user clarity, revenue proximity, and technical defensibility.
